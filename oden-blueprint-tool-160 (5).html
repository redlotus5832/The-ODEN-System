What each node type is for — and when to use it.
Person / Actor
Any individual who plays a role in your investigation — whether confirmed, suspected, or historically significant. Use this for real named people.
Organization
A company, agency, institution, government body, church, or any formal group. The Smithsonian, a police department, a corporation — if it has a name and institutional structure, it's this.
Gap / Unknown ⭑
The most important node type in this methodology. A specific record that should exist in a specific archive — but doesn't. Not "no evidence found." A named document, in a named repository, that is verifiably absent. These are your primary findings.
Event ⭑
A specific, dateable occurrence — an expedition, a death, a publication, a court hearing, a discovery. Events are anchors on the timeline. Connect them to the people, organizations, and documents that surround them.
Location
A place that matters to the investigation — a canyon, a building, an archive, a crime scene, a country of origin. Useful for showing geographic overlap between actors.
Document / Record
A specific physical or digital document — a letter, a permit, a census entry, a photograph, a court filing. Use this when the document itself is a node in your investigation, not just a source you're citing.
Media
A publication, newspaper, broadcast, or media outlet — or a specific article, story, or broadcast that shaped the public record. Use this when media is an actor in the story, not just a source.
Witness / Source
Someone who saw something, reported something, or provided testimony. Different from Person/Actor — this type marks their role as an informant to the record, not just a subject of it.
Unknown / Suspect
An unidentified person — a John Doe, an unnamed source, a person of interest not yet confirmed. Use this to hold a place for someone you know exists but haven't identified.
Relationship / Connection
When the link itself is what you're investigating, not just the people. A business arrangement, a secret correspondence, a known association. Use this when the relationship has its own sources and history.
Financial / Transaction
A payment, grant, contract, inheritance, or money trail. Financial connections often explain relationships that have no other obvious reason to exist.
Legislation / Law
An act, statute, executive order, regulation, or legal ruling that shaped what was possible, permitted, or required. Laws explain why certain records exist — or couldn't.
Scientific Finding
A study, field report, archaeological finding, medical result, or academic conclusion. Use this when a piece of research is itself a node — something that confirmed, denied, or ignored something important.
Family / Lineage
A family relationship, bloodline, or genealogical connection. Useful in genealogy research, estate and inheritance cases, and any investigation where family ties explain access or motive.
Network / Group
An informal group that isn't a formal organization — a social circle, a ring, a movement, an online community, a fraternal lodge. Use this when the group has no official structure but its members act in coordination.
Concept / Theory
An idea, belief, narrative, or framework that's a node in the investigation. A cover story, an official explanation, a conspiracy theory, a doctrine. Use this when an idea itself is something you need to connect to people and evidence.
Object / Artifact
A physical object — a weapon, a photograph, a specimen, a map, a piece of evidence. Use this when the object itself needs to be tracked through the investigation.
Period / Span
A stretch of time that acts as a container — the Reconstruction Era, the missing 18 minutes, the post-merger blackout period. Use this to group events, gaps, and actors that cluster within a specific window.
Alias / Identity
A fake name, pseudonym, username, codename, or assumed identity. Critical in fraud, espionage, true crime, and any case where someone was operating under a different name.
Rumor / Claim
An unverified assertion — a tip, an allegation, an eyewitness claim, a story that's circulating without documentation. Not a gap (that's a missing record) and not a document (that's a paper trail). This is the claim itself, floating without a paper anchor.
Pattern / Behavior
A recurring behavior that's a finding in itself — systematic document destruction, repeated access denial, a modus operandi. Use this when you've identified something that happens more than once and the repetition is the point.

    The Unverifiable Online Methodology — Blueprint

    This is your research roadmap. Work through each phase in order — each one builds on the last. You don't need to complete everything at once. Come back and fill things in as your research develops.

    The goal isn't to prove something happened. It's to document what the record can and cannot tell you — and why. That's a conclusion you can actually defend.

    Original methodology by unverifiableonline.com — 2026.
    PHASE 01
    Who Was There? Map the power landscape
    +

    Before you touch a single archive, figure out who controlled the space around your event. This isn't about guilt — it's about understanding who had the power to create records, control access, shape the narrative, or make things disappear. These people and organizations become your first nodes on the map.

    Think about: Who owned the land? Who ran the press? Who credentialed the science? Who held political power? Who had money at stake?

    Grand Canyon example: Santa Fe Railway (controlled physical access to the South Rim), Ralph Cameron (controlled trail access via mining claims), Fred Harvey (ran lodging — had guest records), Smithsonian Institution (would have credentialed a legitimate discovery), William Randolph Hearst (owned the newspaper that ran the story).

    True crime example: Local police dept (controlled the investigation), county coroner (controlled cause of death finding), DA's office (controlled what got prosecuted), local newspaper (controlled public narrative).

    Genealogy example: Parish church (controlled birth/death/marriage records), local government (controlled land and probate records), immigration authority (controlled entry documentation).
    PHASE 02
    What Records Should Exist? Build your evidence checklist
    +

    If this event really happened, what paperwork would it have generated? This isn't a wishlist — it's a deduction. Organizations in any era follow routines: people file travel vouchers, scientists keep field logs, agencies record permits. Work out what should exist based on how things actually worked at the time.

    Be specific: what type of record, who would have created it, and where it would be held today. These become your Gap nodes if the records turn out to be missing.

    Historical/archival: Travel vouchers (NARA RG 7 — every government employee filed these). Expedition field logs (Smithsonian Archives). Antiquities Act permits (Dept. of Interior). Freight manifests for 700 glass plates.

    True crime: Police incident report. Autopsy or coroner's report. 911 call log. Witness statements. Toxicology results. Chain of custody records for physical evidence.

    Genealogy: Birth certificate (county vital records). Church baptism register. Census entry. Immigration manifest. Naturalization papers. Probate filing.
    PHASE 03
    Go Ask For Them File requests, track responses
    +

    Now actually go to the archives and ask for the records you identified in Phase 2. File formal requests at every repository on your list. This is where the research gets real.

    Log every request you file — the date, where you asked, what you asked for, and what happened. A pattern of non-responses, referrals, or "not found" results is itself evidence. Track it carefully.

    Tip: Use the Documents tab to store anything you actually receive. Come back here to log the request trail.

    Historical/archival: NARA College Park — USDA RG 7, Kincaid travel vouchers 1908–1909 (result: no vouchers found). Smithsonian Archives — BAE expedition records 1909 (result: no Grand Canyon expedition on record).

    True crime: City PD records division — incident reports June–July 1987 (result: two reports found, one missing). County coroner — full autopsy file case #87-441 (result: toxicology pages not included, referred to state lab).

    Genealogy: County vital records — birth certificate for Thomas Kowalski, approx. 1902 (result: no record found). Ellis Island database — immigration manifest search (result: three possible matches, none confirmed).
    PHASE 04
    Build the Map Connect what you've found
    +

    Now use the Nodes & Edges tab to build your visual map. Add everything you've identified in Phases 1–3 as nodes, then draw the connections between them.

    Gap nodes are especially important here. A gap node isn't "no evidence was found" — that's too vague. It's a specific record that should exist, in a specific archive, that is specifically missing. The more precise, the stronger the finding.

    Look for convergence: which people or organizations show up across multiple independent threads? That clustering is the core of the closed system finding.

    Good gap node (historical): "El Tovar 1908 Guest Register — registers survive for 1905–1907 and 1909–1912. The 1908 volume is the only one missing, in the exact year the story was published."

    Good gap node (true crime): "Toxicology Report — autopsy file #87-441 contains all pages except the tox screen. The coroner's index lists it as completed. The pages are gone."

    Good gap node (genealogy): "1905 Iowa State Census — every household on this street is listed except the Kowalski family, who appear in 1900 and 1910."

    Weak gap node (any domain): "No evidence was found." Too vague — it doesn't connect to a specific record in a specific place.
    PHASE 05
    Test Your Method Make sure it's not finding ghosts everywhere
    +

    Before you accept your findings, run the same framework on a case you already know the answer to. Pick something similar — same era, same type of event — but where the outcome is well-documented.

    If your methodology finds the same "closed system" pattern in the control case too, the framework has a flaw. That means you need to fix how you're looking, not declare victory. This step is what separates research from confirmation bias.

    Historical example: Applied the same framework to the Bermuda Triangle — same structure of claimed disappearances and missing evidence. Result: records exist, investigations documented, explanations on file. The methodology found nothing anomalous. Good.

    True crime example: Applied the same framework to a solved murder case from the same precinct, same era. Result: all the records that should exist do exist — incident report, full autopsy, tox screen, chain of custody. No structural gaps. The framework didn't find a closed system there, which means finding one in your case is meaningful.

    Genealogy example: Applied the same search to a neighbor family from the same village, same period. Found census entries, church records, immigration documents — the full paper trail. Their records exist. The absence in your family's case stands out.
    PHASE 06
    State What You Actually Found Honest accounting of the record
    +

    This is your conclusion — but it's not "here's what happened." It's "here is what the documentary record can tell us, here is what it cannot tell us, and here is why that gap exists."

    Be honest about the limits. A finding that says "the record cannot confirm or deny this" is a real finding. It's more defensible than overclaiming, and it's what the methodology is built to produce.

    Welcome to ODEN

    ODEN is a standalone research tool for investigating what the historical, institutional, or archival record can and cannot tell you about a question. It runs entirely in your browser — no account, no server, no internet required after the first load. Everything you enter stays on your computer until you choose to share it.

    The goal isn't to prove a story. It's to find out whether the system that dismissed it ever had the records to back that claim.
    Your First 15 Minutes

    This walkthrough will take you through each part of the tool using a simple example: imagine you're investigating why your grandfather's immigration records from the early 1900s seem to be missing. You've checked the obvious places and come up empty. Now you want to understand why the record has gaps.

    STEP 01
    Name Your Case
    +

    At the top of the screen you'll see a CASE field. Type a name for your investigation — something short that identifies it. This gets saved with your work.

    Example: "Grandfather's Missing Immigration Records — 1903-1910"

    Then click the BLUEPRINT tab to start thinking through your case.

    STEP 02
    Work the Blueprint
    +

    The BLUEPRINT tab is your research roadmap. It walks you through a series of phases — each one asks questions that help you think about your case structurally. You don't need to answer everything at once. Come back as your research develops.

    Start with Phase 1: Who Was There? — this is about mapping who had the power to create, control, or lose records related to your question.

    Example: For immigration records, you'd think about: Which port of entry? What shipping line? What government agency processed arrivals? Was there a church or ethnic community organization that kept parallel records? Who had the power to document — or fail to document — your grandfather's arrival?
    STEP 03
    Add Your First Nodes
    +

    Go to the NODES & EDGES tab. Nodes are the building blocks of your investigation — people, organizations, locations, documents, events, and evidence gaps. Each one gets a colored dot on the map.

    Start by adding the key players from your Blueprint thinking. Give each node a name, pick a type (the color categories you see on the map), and write a short description.

    Example nodes for our case:
    ● Grandfather (Person) — "Arrived at Ellis Island between 1903-1910, exact date unknown"
    ● Ellis Island Processing Center (Organization) — "Federal immigration station, operated 1892-1954"
    ● Missing Ship Manifest Page (Gap) — "Pages 34-38 of the SS Carpathia manifest for Oct 1905 are missing from NARA microfilm"
    ● 1910 Census Entry (Document) — "Shows grandfather living in Brooklyn, lists arrival year as 1905"

    The Gap / Unknown node type is especially important. It marks a specific record that should exist in a specific place — but doesn't. These are your primary findings.

    STEP 04
    Connect Your Nodes
    +

    Still in NODES & EDGES, scroll down to the Add Edge section. Edges are documented connections between nodes — not guesses, but relationships you can source.

    Pick two nodes and describe the relationship. Set a weight (how strong or significant the connection is).

    Example edges:
    Grandfather → Ellis Island — "Processed through Ellis Island per 1910 census declaration"
    Missing Ship Manifest → Ellis Island — "Manifest gap falls within Ellis Island's own record system"
    1910 Census → Grandfather — "Census confirms arrival year, provides Brooklyn address"
    STEP 05
    Check Your Map
    +

    Click the MAP tab. You'll see your nodes as colored dots with lines connecting them. Drag nodes around to organize them. Scroll to zoom. Click any node to see its details in a side panel.

    The map doesn't argue anything — it shows density. When multiple unrelated nodes start connecting to the same point, that's a structural finding. When gaps cluster around a specific institution or time period, that's telling you something about the record itself.

    STEP 06
    Write Your Findings
    +

    The CONTEXT tab is where your actual writing lives. Create sections — these are the chapters of your investigation. Give each one a category and heading, then write up what you've found.

    You can link nodes to your sections so you can always trace a claim back to the evidence on your map.

    Example section:
    Category: BURDEN OF PROOF
    Heading: "The Missing Manifest Pages"
    Content: Your analysis of why those specific pages are missing, what the gap means, and what it does and doesn't prove.
    STEP 07
    Log Documents & Sources
    +

    Use the DOCUMENTS tab to track specific documents you've found or are looking for — letters, records, census entries, photographs. Set their status (received, pending, requested, denied) so you can see at a glance where your document trail stands.

    Use the SOURCES tab to log the archives, libraries, and repositories you're working with — the places the documents come from. Include accession numbers, URLs, and contact info so you can find things again.

    STEP 08
    Test Your Own Work
    +

    The METHODOLOGY CHECK tab asks hard questions about your own research. Are you finding real gaps or just missing things? Could the absence be explained by something mundane? Is your methodology falsifiable — could it produce a result that contradicts your expectations?

    This is what separates research from belief. If your framework can't fail, it's not a framework — it's a confirmation machine. Answer these questions honestly.

    STEP 09
    Save & Import
    +

    Your work saves automatically to your browser as you type. But browsers can lose data if you clear your cache, so use the SAVE YOUR WORK tab to download a .oden file — a backup of your entire investigation. You can open it again anytime on any computer.

    If you have existing research notes, the IMPORT FILES tab can extract names, dates, organizations, and key terms from .txt, .md, .csv, or .docx files and turn them into nodes. Nothing gets added without your approval.

    What You Can Investigate

    ODEN works for any research question where you need to understand the state of the record — not just what's in it, but what's missing and why. Some examples:

    HISTORICAL RESEARCH
    Investigating events where the official record may be incomplete — not to prove a conspiracy, but to establish whether the system that produced the record was capable of documenting what happened.
    GENEALOGY
    Tracing family history through fragmented records across multiple archives and institutions. Mapping which records exist, which are missing, and which institutions controlled the documentation.
    TRUE CRIME & COLD CASES
    Examining whether investigations were thorough by mapping who controlled evidence, who had jurisdiction, and where the documentation trail breaks down.
    JOURNALISM & INVESTIGATIONS
    Building structured, sourceable investigations where every claim connects to documented evidence and every gap is identified as a gap — not assumed to be proof of anything.
    Key Principles

    Nothing goes on the map without a source. If you can't point to a document, record, or verified piece of evidence, it doesn't get a node. The map shows what's documented — not what's suspected.

    Gaps are findings, not proof. A missing record is documented as a missing record. It might mean suppression. It might mean bad filing. It might mean the system was never capable of producing that record in the first place. The gap is the finding — the interpretation is separate.

    The methodology is falsifiable. If you can't imagine a result that would contradict your expectations, you're not doing research. Point the framework at something it should fail on. If it fails cleanly, the tool works. If it finds problems everywhere regardless of the case, the tool is broken.

    Everything stays on your computer. ODEN runs entirely in your browser. No data is sent anywhere. No account is needed. Your investigation is private until you choose to share it.
